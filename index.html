1 The Context: Why South Korea?

~10 min

South Korea is one of the most remarkable stories of democratic transition in modern history. In just a few decades, it went from military dictatorship to a thriving democracy with one of the highest HDIs in the world (0.937 in 2023).

But it didn't happen automatically. Citizens had to fight for it.

How can a non-democratic society become a democracy? And what quality of an ideal society does that reveal?

2 The Presidents: A Timeline of Power

~15 min

Before the 1987 democratic transition, South Korea was ruled by a series of authoritarian leaders. Click each president below to learn how they came to power, how long they ruled, and how they left. Look for patterns.

Syngman Rhee (1948–1960)

How he came to power: Elected as the first president of South Korea in 1948, with strong US support during the early Cold War. He had spent decades in exile in the US lobbying for Korean independence.

How he ruled: Increasingly authoritarian over 12 years. He amended the constitution to extend his presidency, suppressed opposition, and rigged the 1960 election.

How he left power: The April Revolution of 1960 — mass student protests against election fraud forced him to resign. He fled to Hawaii, where he died in exile.

Pattern to notice: Citizens protested and removed him. But democracy didn't last — a military coup followed within a year.

Park Chung Hee (1961–1979)

How he came to power: Military coup in 1961. He seized control from the short-lived democratic government that followed Rhee's removal.

How he ruled: 18 years of authoritarian rule. He drove rapid industrialisation (the "Miracle on the Han River") but crushed political opposition. In 1972 he declared martial law and rewrote the constitution to give himself permanent power (the Yushin Constitution).

How he left power: Assassinated in 1979 by the director of his own intelligence agency during a private dinner. The motive remains debated.

Pattern to notice: Economic development happened under dictatorship. Does a high standard of living require democracy? This connects to the HDI questions from Weeks 1–2.

Chun Doo Hwan (1980–1988)

How he came to power: Another military coup, just months after Park's assassination. He declared martial law and violently suppressed the Gwangju Uprising in May 1980, killing hundreds of pro-democracy protesters.

How he ruled: 8 years of military rule. He continued economic development but maintained strict control over media, opposition parties, and civil liberties.

How he left power: The June 1987 protests (which you'll read about in Step 3) forced his regime to agree to democratic reforms. He left office in 1988 and was later convicted of treason and corruption.

Pattern to notice: The Gwangju massacre was a turning point — it radicalised a generation of Korean citizens and planted the seeds for the 1987 protests.

Roh Tae Woo (1988–1993)

How he came to power: He was Chun's chosen successor and a former military general. But under massive public pressure from the June 1987 protests, he announced the "June 29 Declaration" — agreeing to direct presidential elections, freedom of the press, and other democratic reforms.

How he ruled: South Korea's first democratically elected president (in a genuinely contested election). His presidency was a transitional period — not fully democratic, but far more open than what came before.

How he left power: He completed his term and left office peacefully — the first Korean president to do so. He was later convicted of corruption and accepting bribes.

Pattern to notice: The transition happened because of citizen pressure, but it was also made possible because someone within the system chose to concede. Change required both citizen agency AND structural flexibility.
